A trial usually occurs within one to a couple of weeks, depending on the case and perpetrator. **Influence**
Influence covers the fundamental rules and frame-
work for playing Noir. Everything from action resolu-
tion and how dice should be rolled to drama-creating
mechanisms like fate and drives are covered.
Additionally, how a protagonist is
constructed and the parts included in their
profile are described in detail. Feel free to use the table of contents to
quickly find the rules being sought.
**System**
The system in Noir helps narrator and players
consistently and reasonably determine whether
actions succeed. It exists primarily to
create randomness which in turn creates drama.
In a novel, for example, an action's outcome is
given by the author's desire, while action in role-
playing is based on what participants wish to attempt to perform
in the game world and in the current scene. The system
provides conditions for desired actions to be
executed in a successful way.
**Vision**
Noir's rules system is created according to a vision that
builds on the system being:
• Simple
• Flexible & consistent
• Inspiring
• Reasonable
**Simple**
That the system should be simple means that few steps
should be included in it. Both players and narrator should
quickly understand how the system works and how it should
be interpreted.
Flexible & consistent
By flexibility and consistency is meant that the same com-
pact rules core should recur in all parts of the rules system.
There should be no exceptions or special
solutions for different cases, but the system should be usable
in all situations.
**Inspiring**
It may sound strange to say that a system should be
inspiring — a system is fundamentally just
rules, mathematics and probabilities. By inspiration
is meant in this case that participants should be able to use
the system based on their own characters to create dynamics

<!-- Sida 199 -->

in the story. It's about allowing will over ability
which means that built into the system are elements that make
it possible to produce the utmost, the desperate power
in hopeless situations and use it in dramatic
ways. A competent but willless person performs a given task
worse than a determined amateur. By incorporating
personal gain, conviction and independent influence
in the story's scenes, the engagement and immersion
of the participants will increase, and make the entire experience stronger.
**Reasonable**
One of the fundamental pillars Noir rests on is that
the experience and story take their departure in a reasonable world. To
stop a train by grabbing hold of it is unreasonable, while
stopping a cyclist in the same way is reasonable. However, it should
be pointed out that Noir's world is not the same as our own
and that protagonists can perform feats and/or
heroic deeds that can be seen as unreasonable in our world, often through
sacrifices or thanks to strong conviction. This is
something that both narrator and players quickly learn — to
set the level right for the experience they desire. Some prefer
a greater measure of unreasonableness than others — the important thing is that
actions feel reasonable in the story that's underway.
**Dice**
Dice are used to produce random numbers
which in turn are used to for example answer whether a
person succeeds with their action or how much damage an
attack does.
In Noir, ten-sided dice are used. On certain types of
ten-sided dice the number "0" appears. It should always be interpreted
as "ten" which means that the outcome on a die varies
from one to ten.
To determine whether a person succeeds or fails with
an action, two ten-sided dice are always used. The result
of the roll is obtained by adding together the numbers on
the rolled dice. Generally it applies to roll as high
as possible to increase one's chances of succeeding with the
action.
Dice notations are sometimes abbreviated as 2D, where D
means "dice" and the number indicates the number of dice in the
roll, in this case two.
Maxed & blocked dice
Dice showing ones or tens in the roll have a special
meaning.
For each ten one rolls, one gets to roll another die. This
is called the die being maxed. The number on this new die
is added to the result. If one has the luck to roll new tens
these are also maxed which means one can get very high
results in the end. There is no limit to how many
maxed dice one can get.
For each one rolled, one prevents a maxing. This
is called a block.

**Dice Rolls**
When you want to determine whether a desired action succeeds or
fails, a dice roll is made. There are two types of dice
rolls: difficulty rolls and resistance rolls.
A good general rule for all forms of dice rolls is
to always let the rolled dice lie openly on
the table so that the result can be read. This is because not only the
total value but also the individual dice's
numerical values shall be readable and used for various
purposes. Sometimes the storyteller may also have hidden bonuses
and penalties that are applied to the roll.
When should you use dice rolls?
It's easy to think that you must roll dice in all possible
and impossible situations to see if you succeed with
what you have set out to do — this can include everything from opening
a door to driving a car. This is not the case.
**Dice rolls should be made in the following situations**
• When it is interesting for the scene or story
whether the person succeeds or fails
• If the randomness of the roll contributes to
creating drama or tension.
• If some form of conflict or challenge exists.
• If a person's health and/or survival is at stake.
Rolls should usually not be made in the following situations
• If the situation is routine, boring
or trivial for the person.
• If the randomness would
destroy mood and drama.
• If an action's outcome has already been established in
the scene, new rolls should not be made for the same thing; if
for example a person has succeeded in sneaking past a
guard, then they have succeeded and the stealth skill
should no longer be questioned in the scene.
**Difficulty Rolls**
Difficulty rolls are made when a person must overcome a fixed
difficulty, without direct involvement of any other person.
Driving a car, pushing open a door, climbing over a wall
or finding certain information in a book are typical situations
where difficulty rolls are made.
Difficulty rolls are always made with two dice (2D) and to
this is added a skill's proficiency value or an attribute
value. The result is always compared against a fixed difficulty level
of 20. If the result equals or exceeds 20, the outcome
is counted as successful, if it is below 20 the roll is considered failed.
Which proficiency or attribute value should be used
for the roll is stated in parentheses. "Make a difficulty roll
(Manual Dexterity)" means that the person shall roll 2D and
add the proficiency value in the skill Manual Dexterity.
If this sum equals or exceeds 20, the action
succeeds.
It can happen that a person is so skilled that they
reach 20 automatically even before the dice are rolled. This is
logical since a very skilled individual does not fail at routine
tasks. Rolls can still be made if it is deemed important
to check if the outcome becomes exceptional (see below).
**Resistance Rolls**
Resistance rolls are made when two or more people try
to win over each other through different forms of influence and
conflict. On almost all occasions when a personal skill
is directly pitted against another person's skill,
resistance rolls are used. Common examples of when resistance rolls
are used are during persuasion attempts, gambling, arm
wrestling and attacks in combat.
Resistance rolls are always made with two dice (2D) and
to this is added a skill's proficiency value or an attribute
value. The person who gets the highest result on their roll
succeeds in successfully carrying out their intended action. The one
who got the lower result therefore fails.
Which proficiency or attribute value should be used
for the roll is stated in parentheses. "Make a resistance roll
(Strength)" means that each of those involved shall roll
2D and add the proficiency value in the skill Strength.
The one who gets the highest sum is considered to have succeeded with the roll and
the current action.
In cases where two or more parties reach the same sum,
the one with the highest proficiency or attribute value wins.
If the value is the same, the one who has the highest
value on any of the dice wins. If even this is equal,
each party rolls 1D and whoever rolls highest wins over the other
— if equal, the die is rerolled until the result differs.
**Exceptional Rolls**
An exceptional roll results in something special occurring,
something extraordinary that affects the person's future in
some way. Exceptional rolls can influence either positively
or negatively and can occur with both difficulty rolls
and resistance rolls.
A roll counts as exceptional if the first two dice
in the roll show the same number — any maxed
dice don't count.
If a roll counts as successful and is also exceptional,
it is called an exceptionally successful roll and
something positive occurs beyond the action succeeding.
If a roll counts as failed and is also exceptional,
it is called an exceptionally failed roll and
something negative occurs beyond the action failing.
Exactly what happens during an exceptional roll depends on
what the roll concerns; generally it is up to the storyteller and
player to dramatically and engagingly describe an event
that is reasonable in the scene. In combat it is defined that
it is the person's initiative that is affected, which is described for
each combat action.

**Collaboration**

Multiple people can collaborate when a roll is to be made, if
the task is such that more participants improve the chance
of success. Examples of actions where it is advantageous to
have multiple people are when trying to break down a door, pull a
person up on a rope, disarm a complicated explosive charge
or search for information in books. A typical situation where
it doesn't help to have multiple people is when driving.

Each person who helps with an action, and who
is considered qualified within the action's area, gives the
person who will make the roll (usually the person who has
the highest skill in the relevant ability) a bonus (+2).
The narrator must determine how many people can reasonably
help at the same time — even if there are
twenty people available, not all can break down the
blocked door simultaneously.

**Actions**

The term action is used in all cases where a person wants
to attempt to do something. An action can be more or
less challenging, but some form of reasonable risk of
failure must exist. Actions' outcomes are determined via
the result of either an opposed roll or one or more
difficulty rolls.

Each protagonist has 9 attributes and 27 abilities.
It is usually with the help of skill in an ability that
a roll is made. In some cases rolls are made with the help of attribute values.
In most cases it is obvious which skill or att-
ribute value is most appropriate. If it is unclear, the narra-
tor and player discuss their way to what applies.

Noir's system is not built for interaction between abilities
and/or attributes. All rolls are made with the help of one value
— either a skill value or an attribute value. In spe-

or attribute values may be needed in sequence to determine a result.

The players and/or narrator may also determine that
personal influence can help or hinder an action
— character traits, motivations and other things can influence the
action's result positively or negatively.

**Defense Abilities**

The abilities Resist, Resilience and Tolerance constitute a person's
defense against social, mental and physical attacks respectively. When
opposed rolls to withstand influence are made, the victim
must add the value in the appropriate defense ability to the result.

Resist is used as defense in social conflicts,
for example seduction, interrogation or persuasion attempts. Resil-
ience is used as defense against horror experiences, stress or
violations. Tolerance is used as defense against adverse
climate, drug influence or poisoning.

**Perception Abilities**

The abilities Awareness, Intuition and Empathy are typical
perception abilities — how well a person perceives things in
their surroundings. Awareness is the five common senses
— sight, hearing, touch, taste and smell. Intuition is the sixth
sense, a subconscious sense that interprets information
intuitively and can provide clues or premonitions about what
is to come. Empathy opens a person's emotional channel so that
he/she can sense and read other people's general
mood — for example sadness or anger.

Often the narrator makes rolls with the protagonists' percep-
tion abilities secretly, to avoid unnecessarily giving players
insight that they might be missing something.

**Luck**

The attribute Luck shows a person's unconscious flow in existence and
general luckiness in situations where skill is sub-

Social Interaction,
Reputation & Relationships
To survive in a cold and harsh world, it's important to have contacts and friends you can turn to for help. Otherwise, you often learn the hard way that alone is weak.

**Social Interaction**
The interaction between player characters and narrator characters is a vital part of any roleplaying story. Many scenes consist of various forms of dialogue, whether it be persuasion, threats, or perhaps seduction. These are collectively called social conflicts as they involve two wills struggling to achieve their respective goals.

This section describes the procedures used in social conflicts between player characters, and between player characters and narrator characters. The rules are designed to create drama and to give player characters who are skilled at the social game the advantages they deserve.

**Conducting Social Situations**
There are essentially three different ways to act out social interaction, and none is really more right or wrong - different gaming groups prefer different methods.

The first method involves no dice being used at all; all dialogue takes place between the players and/or the narrator - in other words, what a participant says becomes what their player character or narrator character says in the story. This method takes very little notice of the character's numerical values, and the role is replaced, so to speak, by the person behind the character and their social and verbal competence.

The second method is to roll dice for appropriate skills - whoever wins succeeds at what they set out to do and the scene is over. This is how most actions in Noir work, from combat and driving to picking locks or fixing a broken engine, so why shouldn't Persuade and Charm work the same way? However, this method is far from ideal. Social interaction is important in roleplaying - the form of roleplaying is made for conversation and for the entertainment, joy, and drama it brings when participants together build atmospheres, crystallize characters, and move the story forward.

What remains then is the third method: combining methods one and two. This is the approach Noir advocates and that the rules best support. In this case, the social conflict begins with

<!-- Page 205 -->

the players delivering the arguments their player characters would have presented in the story, and the narrator does the same with their narrator characters. When it feels appropriate, resistance rolls are made to determine who gains the upper hand in the conflict.

If one or more parties have succeeded in providing good, exciting, sharp arguments during the opening, this should be rewarded with a bonus to the roll. Factors such as character traits and/or motivations can also have an effect, and naturally any relationship between the parties should also be factored into the whole.

**The Social Conflict**
The purpose of social conflicts is that they are indeed conflicts to be overcome. A conflict can vary in scope from small to large, from personal to universal. Almost all social situations in Noir can be seen as social conflicts.

**Parties**
In every social conflict there is an active and a passive party. The active party uses some appropriate skill - usually Charm or Persuade - to achieve their goal, see table 1:38. The passive party, that is, the one being targeted, usually uses the skill Resist. In situations where violence, or the threat of violence, is involved, the passive party can use the skill Resilience instead.

If there is any doubt about who is active and who is passive in a social conflict, the rule of thumb is that whoever has the highest attribute value in Impression is the active party.

In the rare situations where there is no passive party - this occurs primarily in pure debate situations - both parties roll with skills as if active.

**The Stakes**
In certain social conflicts it seems natural that the active party must choose what the goal of the conflict is. Or rather, what happens if the goal is achieved. This is called the stakes. In the same way, the passive party should propose what the effect will be if the active party fails to gain the upper hand and win the conflict. Both stakes should be roughly equally serious - so that an attempt to win big means that a failure also becomes significant.

For example, a failed negotiation at a critical stage might mean the active party is ostracized or dismissed, successful mingling grants an audience with the countess while a failure means dishonor upon the character and their family, and attempting to sing high C can mean anything from fantastic to catastrophic reviews in the culture sections the next day.

Example: Gabriel and Mercurian are both in love with a mysterious femme fatale named Yvonette. Gabriel wants to get Mercurian to back off and not interfere with his continued courtship of Yvonette. The stakes for Gabriel are proposed to be "get Mercurian to stop loving Yvonette." In the discussion between players, however, the stakes are judged to be skewed - it's not something a social conflict can decide, but something deeper. Instead, the stakes are redefined to the clearer "get Mercurian to stay away from Yvonette for now."

The effect if Gabriel loses becomes "Gabriel becomes uncertain of himself regarding his chances with Yvonette compared to Mercurian's." Exactly what that would mean can be judged in future scenes featuring the three involved - perhaps Gabriel gets a penalty on the courtship, or maybe Mercurian can play out the discussion they had to make Gabriel angry, broken, or sad.

**Social Conflicts Between Player Characters**
Player characters can also be active and passive in a social conflict. This means that one player character can influence another according to the rules above. A player has no general veto, but if an influence goes strongly against a character's personality, the resistance roll should be modified with bonuses or penalties to symbolize this.

**Resolution**
Whoever gets the highest result on the resistance roll stands as the victor in the conflict. If the one who didn't want to be persuaded (the passive party) wins in an argument about whether Sayah Bonn is the world's most beautiful woman, it means the arguments didn't bite and the active party must accept that they were wrong. If the one who initiated the conflict (the active party) wins, this means the opponent accepts and agrees that Bonn is indeed the world's most beautiful woman.

Note that a victory doesn't necessarily mean the loser changes their attitude about what the conflict concerned, but only that the winner's arguments trumped the loser's.

A win in a social conflict is lasting for at least the current scene and at most until further notice, depending on the situation and the stakes - people are by nature changeable and can change their minds at the most inopportune times.

A social conflict can escalate, see below, if the losing party doesn't accept the outcome.

If a party during a social conflict gets an exceptional result on the resistance roll, the character gets either a bonus (+5) or a penalty (-5) on all further rolls in the same social conflict - this is important during escalation.

**Escalation**
The losing party in a social conflict has two alternatives. The first, and perhaps most common, is to accept the defeat and act according to the winner's goal, based on any stakes. The second is to escalate the situation in hopes of a changed outcome.

Every social conflict has four levels of severity: Conversation, Quarrel/squabble, Brawl/fistfight, and Deadly violence.

Conversation: The parties discuss in normal tones and present their positions, wishes, or wills with respectful and logical arguments.

Quarrel/squabble: The situation is harsher in tone. Loud exchanges of opinion, crude insults, hurtful personal attacks, verbal blows below the belt, shoves, slaps, or blustering gestures close to the opponent's person are included.

Brawl/fistfight: The situation degenerates and the discussion turns into non-lethal violence - such as wild fistfight, sneaky cheap shots, throws and holds, chaotic wrestling match, or storming close combat with all manner of improvised weapons. However, the intent is rather to defeat the opponent than to kill them.

Deadly violence: A raging fight to the death breaks out. The parties don't hesitate to attack each other with merciless violence, with everything from melee weapons to firearms, with the intent to kill. Uninhibited combat is a fact.

Whoever is active in a social conflict determines which escalation level the whole thing starts at - either Conversation or Quarrel/squabble. When the loser decides not to accept but to escalate the conflict, they can go to whichever escalation level is desired, as long as it's more serious than the current one. Then the winner can choose to follow to the new level or abstain and hand over the victory to the loser.

To my admirer Adrian Niemans, thank you for your support and for the beautiful flowers. You know how to make a film star happy.

If the victor follows to the new level, a new opposed roll between the parties shall be made and the loser gets to decide whether to either accept or escalate the conflict further, as far as is possible. Which abilities are used for the roll are judged case by case depending on circumstances and situation.

At the levels Brawl/fight and Lethal violence, a party can at any time choose to give victory to their opponent by giving up and accepting defeat.

The point of the escalation rules is to give the player good opportunities to participate in the conflict and steer it forward if desired. Most often escalation means that some form of cost arises in the form of enmity, lost face, missed opportunity, visit to jail, or in the worst case, that someone dies. Sometimes it can be considered worth paying that cost to win a conflict.

Example: Mordekai has picked up the scent of one of those responsible for his beloved's death. The person is at the opera when the rain-soaked Mordekai throws open the door to the foyer. At the door is a snooty opera guard who informs Mordekai that he must wait until intermission as no admittance is allowed after the performance has begun.

This is a typical social conflict, where Mordekai as the active party tries to persuade the guard to let him in. Mordekai sets the level to "Conversation" as he doesn't want to brawl openly and draw attention from the other opera employees. The opposed rolls are made with Convince versus Resist and the guard gets a higher result than Mordekai.

Mordekai can now choose to accept not being let in, or he can escalate the conflict — which he does! He pulls it straight up to "Brawl/fight" and simply decides he wants to knock down the opera guard, who chooses to follow along in the escalation. This means the social conflict becomes a fight where Mordekai fairly quickly gains the upper hand and wins. The guard, as loser, chooses to accept without escalating to the final level: "Lethal violence".

The cost in the given situation is that other staff have contacted the police to remove Mordekai from the opera.

**Special social situations**

Certain forms of social conflicts require a more detailed explanation. See below.

**Interrogate**

To interrogate someone means that through psychological attrition, frightening threats, fair promises or with brutal violence one attempts to force information out of someone. The one conducting the interrogation is always active and the one being interrogated is passive. Each opposed roll is calculated to take approximately one hour to complete.

If the active party fails, their Wellbeing decreases (−1) due to frustration. If the active party succeeds, the passive party suffers stress corresponding to a shocking experience, or, if much violence, threats and torture have occurred, a psychological trauma. When the passive party's Wellbeing reaches Tormented (2) or lower, the passive party's defenses break and the active party learns all reasonable information they requested.

The active party can affect their roll. By using torture they get a bonus (+5). If the active party possesses information that affects the passive party on a personal level — for example real threats against family/relatives, threats of revealing something the interrogated doesn't want to come out, or similar — they get a bonus (+5). Each doubling of interrogation time gives a bonus (+2), up to a maximum of (+10).

**Haggle over price**

When a buyer tries to haggle down the price of goods, an opposed roll is made as usual for a social conflict — the buyer is always active and the seller always passive.

If the buyer succeeds, the price is lowered by five percentage points times the number on the lowest die in the roll. If the buyer fails, the price is not adjusted. When haggling, usually no direct stake is made, as it is considered a universal phenomenon in Sandukar and the Empire. Those who don't try to haggle are viewed with skepticism. This also means the seller usually sets a higher price on their goods than what they expect to actually get.

**Threaten**

By acting threateningly, a person can frighten another person into doing things they don't really want to. The one making the threat is always active while the target is passive. If the opposed roll succeeds, the passive party is exposed to stress, corresponding to a shocking experience, which is described on page 244.

The relationship automatically worsens one step when threats are used, regardless of whether the conflict is escalated or not.

**Inspirational speech**

A leader can make a mentally worn subordinate, soldier or henchman feel better through an inspirational speech. The one speaking is always active while the target is passive. If the opposed roll succeeds, the passive party's Wellbeing improves one level, if it fails it worsens one level. The passive party's Wellbeing may at best be Gloomy (6) for the speech to have any effect.

At most one attempt may be made per game session and person.

**Bribe**

By bribing a person with money, objects or advantages, a person can reach set goals more easily or escape tragic situations. The one bribing is always active and the recipient of the bribe is passive. If the opposed roll succeeds, the passive party is bribed and performs what the active party requests. The relationship automatically worsens one step when bribes are used, as respect is eroded. If the same person is bribed multiple times, the relationship is not lowered again however.

Generally a bribe should correspond to at least half a monthly salary (for the passive party) for minor activities, and one or more monthly salaries for more serious ones. Situation and circumstances — such as the consequences the passive party perceives they could suffer if the activity is discovered or how great the risk is of being caught — must determine how large the bribe should be. If too low sums are offered, the active party gets a penalty, while higher amounts provide a bonus. In the Empire everyone is corruptible if the sum is right.

**The importance of game flow**

Defining the stakes — what happens both with a successful and with a failed result — before the dice are actually rolled can be double-edged. At best it leads to everyone participating in the conflict knowing what's at stake, accepting it and moving forward with what they intended to do.

On the other hand, this defining can initiate a long discussion about what is reasonable and what is not, which in turn leads to players wanting to revise the outcome at for example a success in order to thereby change the result of a possible failure. Such discussions can become very drawn out and destroy the flow of the game and the total experience for everyone involved.

**To avoid unnecessary** game interruptions, the narrator can introduce penalties to the opposed roll for each time a player changes their mind. This can be explained in the story by the protagonist seeming nervous or otherwise uncertain about their case and their arguments. Another tool for the game master to maintain game flow is to only let the players define what they want to happen with a successful outcome and only when the dice are rolled explain what happens with a possible failure.

**Seduce**

"If you want someone to love you, open your heart. If you want someone to become obsessed with you, close it."
— Talia Navarre

Seduction is the art of enchanting someone to do something for you. Generally the relationship worsens one step if the opposed roll fails for the active party.

One of the most common stakes is that the active party simply tries to get the passive party in bed, to have sex or for some other reason.

Another common stake is to create an attraction. This means the active party tries to get the victim interested, lured, focused and tempted, through promises, hints and flirtation. This is done through an opposed roll (Charm). If the active party succeeds, they get until further notice a general bonus (+2) on all rolls in such social interactions with the victim where it is judged that the attraction can facilitate. Note that it can be completely non-sexual interactions — once the attraction is awakened, it is.

The active party can try to strengthen an attraction through another successful opposed roll so it becomes a passion. If this succeeds, the bonus becomes (+5) instead.

Both attraction and passion are noted in the character sheet, next to the relationship.

Note that the seduction doesn't need to be of a sexual nature at all — a leader who captivates underlings or an artist who succeeds in charming an admirer follow the same rules.

An attraction or passion needs to be maintained to persist — if the active party ignores or humiliates the victim too much, a passion can drop to become an attraction and an attraction can cease entirely. Often this results in hurt feelings and bitterness on the victim's part.

As usual, if a victim is willing, no rolls need to be made at all. The same applies if the victim lacks capacity to be seduced, for example if a man tries to pick up a homosexual woman, if a woman wants to have sex with an impotent man, etcetera. However, it is entirely possible for attraction or passion to arise even if an anything but benign relationship exists between two individuals — even enemies can be attracted to each other.

A protagonist has a defined relationship with all narrator characters he/she knows. Each such narrator character also has an influence that shows his/her power within the field he/she operates in.

When a protagonist is created relationships are established, see page 29. **Impression**
Impression shows how a person is perceived and received by others at first meeting. Each time a protagonist enters a social conflict with a new narrator character a difficulty roll (Impression) must be made to see how the protagonist is perceived by the narrator character. The result gives the relationship the protagonist has with the narrator character during the social conflict and until further notice.

Note that even if several protagonists are part of a group it is the one who primarily acts in the social conflict who creates the relationship – the other protagonists do not get a relationship with the narrator character.

Exceptionally successful roll: The relationship is set to Benevolent.
Successful roll: The relationship is set to Neutral.
Failed roll: The relationship is set to Disliking.
Exceptionally failed roll: The relationship is set to Hostile.

The difficulty roll can be modified depending on whether the narrator character has any preconceived opinions that could affect it. A person who for example hates police and who is interrogated by a constable gives a penalty to the constable's difficulty roll. Other things to consider here could be factors such as racism, rivalry, possible threats, prejudices, previous experiences, or general behavior by the person before the difficulty roll (Impression) is made.

A narrator character never makes an Impression roll to create a relationship with a protagonist – the protagonist's player decides for themselves how he/she perceives the narrator character based on the narrator's portrayal.

**Relationship**
A person is rarely alone in the darkness of the city. Turning to others and sharing joy, experiences and companionship is a way to resist the heavy and inhumane environment the city represents. **Friendship**
In Noir's world unconditional friendship is something rare. It means that the narrator character has a deep emotional bond with the protagonist and supports almost everything he/she needs help with, even if it is risky or dangerous. This level of relationship usually means that the narrator character loves or is deeply in love with the protagonist, or that they are very close friends since long ago.

**Benevolent**
The narrator character is benevolently disposed toward the protagonist and can consider helping out with time, information or material help without major demands for compensation. To get the narrator character to help with something that entails danger to his/her life or that constitutes a great risk however requires very good reasons or proper encouragement.

**Neutral**
The narrator character sees the protagonist as an acquaintance, someone he/she knows but has no special relationship with. The narrator character can politely help with simpler tasks or questions, but more advanced or dangerous proposals will probably result in the narrator character either breaking off the conversation or demanding good compensation. The narrator character may very well sell out the protagonist if such a situation should present itself, as there is no loyalty between the two.

**Disliking**
The narrator character probably sees the protagonist as an opponent. If this is the case the narrator character does not hesitate to put down, harass or humiliate the protagonist. It can even go so far that the narrator character actively tries to sabotage for the protagonist, not to inflict physical harm but rather to mess with him/her.

At lower influence levels it involves a lone individual or a smaller group of enemies, for example a small street gang or perhaps a couple of corrupt constables. Influential enemies are significantly more well-organized and have good equipment. At very high degrees of influence the opponent has very great power, he/she can for example be gang boss, member of a House, or similar.

**Hostile**
The narrator character is hostilely disposed toward the protagonist and does not hesitate to harm, maim or kill him/her if the situation should become serious. The protagonist should see the narrator character as a real threat if their paths should cross.

Sometimes the narrator character may seek out the protagonist themselves to set him/her up in various ways. It is a matter of pure and simple hatred, disgust, emotional bitterness or perhaps cold vindictiveness due to a real or fabricated wrong. An enemy does not hesitate to go after their victim's family, friends and acquaintances so everyone is in danger when the enemy is near.

At lower degrees of influence it involves a lone individual who has no direct significance but who can still be life-threatening. An influential enemy has a collection of henchmen, great resources or is very skilled at what he/she does. At very high influence levels it involves a very influential, rich, or powerful person, who spends time finding their victim and killing him/her.

**Changing an established relationship**
An established relationship may very well change. Most common is that such things occur naturally in a story. The narrator must judge whether it is reasonable for the relationship to change based on the experiences the protagonist and narrator character share. If the protagonist for example sets up or makes life harder for the narrator character the relationship can deteriorate.

The relationship can change as many steps as necessary – a scorned lover may very well plummet from "Friendship" to "Hostile" if the protagonist has betrayed and deeply hurt him/her. In the same way a person who initially disliked the protagonist can after a time discover that the first impression was wrong and become "Benevolently" disposed.

A relationship does not change more than once per scene unless special reasons exist.

Resilience is an important ability for commando soldiers, spies, smugglers, waitresses, soldiers, prostitutes,
police officers, investigators, and informants.

Mobility (Coordination)
Mobility describes a person's trained movement ability,
grace, sense of rhythm, and bodily flexibility, agility, suppleness,
and springiness. The concept also includes a certain form of balanced strength required for utilizing the body's sway
and power in, for example, long jumps or free climbing. The
ability is also used for bodily maneuvers such as acrobatic feats or advanced body contortions. Mobility
also provides talent for more shady activities such as moving
quietly or sneaking past others without being noticed.
Mobility is an important ability for burglars, gymnasts, athletes, climbers, acrobats, detectives, scouts,
commando soldiers, spies, dancers, strippers, and performers.

Composure (Will)
Composure describes a person's self-discipline, dignity, and calm.
A composed person is often methodical, controlled, balanced,
and unaffected. Often the person possesses good self-confidence
and is fundamentally secure. In stressed or surprising
situations, those with good aptitude in the ability
enjoy good opportunities to act relaxed, rationally, and calculatedly.
The person can still crack and break down if
the mental stress becomes too great, even if they
might still manage to hide it from the world
by maintaining an external calm and dignity.
Composure is an important ability for martial artists, stressed careerists, wise hermits, lifestyle personalities,
soldiers, shadow hunters, cult leaders, slick criminals, and
con artists.

Create (Creativity)
Create indicates a person's aesthetic development and talent.
The ability is used when a person wants to write, compose
music, paint, sculpt, photograph, or design and sew
clothes. Create can also be used for more shady activities such as forging licenses, papers,
and documents. Within the ability there is also knowledge of
makeup, cosmetics, wigs, and disguise.
Create is an important ability for artists, journalists,
writers, actors, makeup artists, poets, musicians,
photographers, entertainers, and designers.

Firearms (Motor Skills)
Firearms is a person's ability to correctly handle, control, and fire firearms of various kinds, from light handguns to heavy sniper rifles and machine guns, but for
deepening knowledge there are numerous different expertises.
This includes familiarity with how to load, unsecure, perform
bolt action, etc. Knowledge of weapon models,
basic weapon mechanism theory, ammunition knowledge,
and weapon care is also included in the ability. Firearms provides certain
insight into how to act in combat situations.
Firearms is an important ability for soldiers, police officers,
snipers, assassins, hitmen, gangsters, gang members, detectives, bodyguards, security personnel, and guards.

Deduction (Creativity)
Deduction is the brain's ability to see connections and
patterns and figure out seemingly nonexistent links
through inference, reasoning, and logical thinking. It

thoughts and ideas. The ability is used to draw conclusions,
solve puzzles, crack ciphers and codes, interpret dreams and
texts, or to progress in various experiments.
Deduction is very common in the police force, especially among
forensic technicians in evidence analysis at crime scenes.
Deduction is an important ability for forensic technicians,
criminal investigators, detectives, scientists, researchers, seers,
criminals, doctors, and tacticians.

Strength (Build)
Strength is a person's muscle mass and physical development,
and a measure of both raw strength and strength technique. The higher
value a person has in this ability, the more well-built
and muscular they are. The ability is important for breaking
down barriers, lifting heavy things, dragging objects, or smashing
something or someone.
Strength is an important ability for boxers, weightlifters,
models, bodybuilders, bouncers, soldiers, fighters,
hitmen, and laborers.

Tolerance (Build)
Tolerance is the body's ability to resist foreign organisms, poisons, and drugs, as well as influence from cold and heat.
Tolerance includes immune defense, metabolism, and general
resistance—and of course tolerance to alcohol.
Tolerance is an important ability for athletes, survival
experts, soldiers, alcoholics, and vagrants.

Awareness (Perception)
Awareness is a person's observational ability and
vigilance and is used both actively and passively. It gives
the person an aptitude for discovering and noting things
by using and interpreting sensory impressions in the best
way in every situation. The ability primarily concerns sight and hearing, but also touch, taste, and smell. It can also be used when searching persons, as well as to stay hidden
and hide oneself.
Awareness is an important ability for scouts, spies, bouncers, bodyguards, soldiers, police officers, snipers, detectives, forensic technicians, burglars, guards, and hunters.

Streetwise (Insight)
Streetwise indicates the ability to function in social contexts. It constitutes a person's social agility and understanding
of how the norms and culture that prevail should be exploited
for best results. A person with Streetwise doesn't feel
lost or out of place in social situations,
regardless of circumstances or social class, but finds ways
to fit in. The ability provides a sense of how to interact
with people of various kinds, how to adapt language use,
jargon, gestures, and conversation topics so as not to offend or show lack of respect for the person you're speaking with. The
ability also includes various specialized areas within social
contexts, for example gambling. Streetwise is also
used to pick up and exploit information "on the street,"
knowing who to talk to about a certain question, or
which bar is the hangout for a certain gang, etc.
Streetwise is an important ability for gossip journalists, socialites, celebrities, spies, luxury prostitutes,
intelligence agents, fixers, fences, undercover cops, con artists,
gamblers, and models.

Persuade (Charisma)
Persuade is the ability to verbally convey a message

such as shyness or awkwardness. There are countless ways to achieve
results, and the ability includes diplomacy, rhetoric, and
negotiation technique as well as pure persuasive ability. The
ability also covers interview and interrogation technique.
Persuade is an important ability for businessmen, reporters,
leaders, mediators, negotiators, interrogators, prostitutes,
salespeople, hitmen, spies, businessmen, detectives, and informants.

Damage Modifier is a person's ability to inflict extra damage on the enemy in close combat. The attribute value is added to all close combat attacks' damage value.
The attribute value for Damage Modifier is obtained by adding together the person's skill in Strength with their Size and dividing the sum by ten (/10), rounded down.
**Size**
Size measures a person's physical bulk, presence, height and weight. The attribute value in Size affects a person's capacity to withstand damage. It is extremely rare that rolls are made directly for Size, instead the value is primarily used in calculations of other values. Once size is determined, it is very rare for it to change — certain expertises can, however, affect it over time.
The attribute value for Size is normally 15, when the person is considered to be medium-sized. The value can be changed during the creation of the person's traits, see page 87.
**Combat Movement**
Combat Movement indicates the person's aptitude for maneuvering their body in a pressing situation such as combat. The concept includes things like not hesitating, moving in a zigzag, keeping the head low and running in a crouched position. In cases where distance measurement is needed in combat, the attribute value in Combat Movement counts as the number of meters the person reaches. This occurs primarily in connection with the Initiative actions Move, Fight and Fury Attack (see page 255).
The attribute value for Combat Movement is obtained by adding together the skill in Determination, Fitness and Agility and dividing the sum by three (/3), rounded down.
**Wound Threshold**
Wound Threshold indicates how resilient a person is and their capacity to take damage without receiving Wounds. If an attack's damage effect exceeds the victim's Wound Threshold they receive a Wound, see page 262 for more information. No rolls are made against the attribute value for Wound Threshold, it is only used as a comparison value.
The attribute value for Wound Threshold is obtained by adding together the skill in Fitness and Strength with the person's Size and dividing the sum by three (/3), rounded down.

Appearance is a person's physical exterior, features, bearing and beauty — or lack thereof. A good appearance helps the person in most cases of social interaction, debate and seduction. Wellbeing shows a person's general health, energy level, freshness, mental soundness and general capacity for action and initiative. Protagonists learn from experiences, maturity, adventures and events. This is called gaining Experience. Experience is used to improve a protagonist's qualities.
Experience & Life Experience
After each story that is concluded, each protagonist who has participated receives a sum of Experience. This sum is added to any previously recorded Experience in the character sheet. The same sum is also added to Life Experience.
**The total sum of Life Experience shows** how much Experience the protagonist has accumulated in total during all their adventures, and can be seen as a general indication of wisdom and knowledge, see table 1:45. It is a good sum for the narrator to use if the protagonists are at very different development levels and a narrator wants a newly arrived protagonist to catch up, then the narrator can award the inexperienced protagonist a certain sum of Experience directly after the protagonist's creation process has been completed.
Experience shows how many available "points" a protagonist has to improve themselves. When a protagonist improves, the sum in Experience is reduced by the cost of the improvement. Life Experience does not decrease when Experience is used, nor can one use Life Experience to improve oneself.
