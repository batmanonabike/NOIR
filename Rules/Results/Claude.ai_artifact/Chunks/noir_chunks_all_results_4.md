**Learning**
When a protagonist uses an ability and gets an
exceptional result (whether successful or failed),
they may place a learning mark next to the ability
on the character sheet. This is also called marking the ability.
A learning mark makes it easier for a protagonist to
increase the ability's ability value by one level using Experience,
see page 222.
Only exceptional rolls for abilities grant learning
marks. Exceptional rolls for attributes grant no marks.
Learning marks remain with the ability until the
ability value is raised, regardless of how long this takes. An ability
can only have one learning mark at a time.

**How a protagonist gains experience**
After a completed story, the narrator should distribute Experience
to the participants. This is preferably done when the protagonists
have an opportunity to take it easy and reflect on
what has occurred in their lives and what they have learned.
Experience is distributed based on the story's Challenge. If
the story has been challenging, difficult, and given the protagonists
opportunity to grow and become an active part of the environment,
this gives more Experience than if it had been simple, routine,
and isolated. The points for Challenge are then multiplied
by the protagonist's attribute value in Learning.
Beyond this sum, extra experience can be awarded
based on Ego – if a player has portrayed their protagonist
in accordance with their personality, character traits,
and motivations, and if they have actively supported and
created exciting and dramatic situations during the story's
course, they can be rewarded with extra points.
The sum of all this becomes the number of Experience Points
distributed to the protagonist. (Formula for Experience:
(Learning x Challenge) + Ego).

**Experience from motivations**
A protagonist can receive Experience upon fulfilling
motivations. The Experience is then distributed directly when the motivation
is fulfilled and used in the same way as other Experience.
For more information about Experience from motivations,
see page 233.

**Experience pacing**
If Experience is distributed rarely, the protagonists should
receive extra much when it does happen. However, it's recommended
to distribute Experience fairly often since the protagonists'
development then becomes more consistent and not so
jerky. Additionally, this provides room to improve Fate continuously,
something that can be crucial for the protagonists'
lives and health.

**When may experience be used?**
Experience may be used when a protagonist is rested,
is in an undisturbed place and doesn't feel stressed,
for example after having slept through an uneventful night.
The protagonist should feel calm and reflective and
have opportunity to gather their thoughts. This can occur multiple times
during one and the same story. Every time Experience
is distributed, it can also be used immediately.
However, Experience may not be used for things like in
the middle of a scene in the story increasing an ability the player just
discovered they needed, or improving Fate just when the need
presents itself.
Note that there are no limitations on
how much unused Experience a protagonist may have.
The player decides themselves when they want to use the protagonist's
Experience for improvements.

**Learning**
The attribute Learning shows a person's general aptitude,
learning ability, and wisdom. It shows how the person primarily
learns things – the hard and physical way,
by reasoning logically and rationally, or by
having an innate cunning and a good deal of guile. More about Learning
can be found on page 218.

**Character Traits in Combat**
Personal conviction can move mountains. A strong
conviction, a burning love, or a seething hatred
can drive people to perform actions that would otherwise
be seen as impossible. A father can run through a
burning house to save his daughter, a woman can drag
her injured husband for miles to reach a hospital,
and a friend can throw themselves into icy water to pull up their
comrade who just fell in.
Both protagonists and prominent narrator characters
have character traits that help define their
personalities, motivations and life goals, but also their
weaknesses and desires. In combat, these character traits can help
or hinder the execution of a plan. If a player's
protagonist has, for example, the character trait Loving
with expression toward his beloved, he can in a situation
where his fiancée is directly threatened choose to add his value in
the character trait as a bonus to a dice roll that
results in the threat becoming less.

A narrator must decide how much
the character traits factor into combat. Ideally, bonuses from
character traits should only be used when something truly matters
in a person's life that they care about. It should not
be used in every other combat situation, but saved
for more climactic events so that the result becomes
incredibly spectacular — like when the antagonist lets their hatred pour
out over the hero, when the protagonist saves their beloved from
a hail of bullets with the help of their strong faith in their love, or
when a companion's loyalty is so strong that it affects
the outcome of a firefight.

Suggestions for use in general combat situations
It's not unthinkable that a player wants to use a person's character trait in just about every combat — especially if
the protagonist is Aggressive, Risk-prone or Cautious.
A simple approach is to let the character trait be
"active" throughout the entire combat. If, for example, Aggressive or
Risk-prone is used, bonuses are given on all Initiative actions
and penalties on all Defense actions, while Cautious
works the opposite way.

**A Drive's Birth**
During a story's course, situations arise that give birth to
drives. When such a situation reveals itself, a person may
choose to take up the drive as their own, provided that he/she
has room for it – see Impact. If not, the person can attempt
to abandon an existing drive. This is described under
Abandon Drive.

When a drive is born, this should be roleplayed by the player,
so that the drive becomes part of the story and something worth
striving for. Everyone should understand why a drive comes
into being and understand the person's motivation for the drive.

All new drives are written down in the character sheet and
should also be explained. Generally, a maximum of one drive
is born per scene, but variations can occur in special cases.

When a person is created, he/she gets one drive, see
page 31.

Stelia Lock" and "Destroy everyone who was involved in the mur-

when Miss Lock is found, the first drive is fulfilled, and when
everyone who was part of the conspiracy that led to Mordekai's
youthful love Janya's death is dead, the second drive is fulfilled.

**Scope**
A drive's scope can be either limited or continuous. Most are
limited, which means they disappear when they are fulfilled.
Continuous drives remain even after they are fulfilled; this
mainly applies to drives whose goal is long-term or can be
affected over a long time, for example protecting a person.

Example: Mordekai Remus has two drives, according to the exam-
ple above. The drives are both considered limited since there is no
reason to keep them once they have been fulfilled. If the second
drive were such that the goal itself was each individual's death,
then it would have been a continuous drive, but now the wording
is such that it is at the point when all involved are dead that the
goal is fulfilled and thus the drive is limited.

A manic drive takes over the person's ego and steers him/her toward the goal day after day, night after night, hour after hour. Conviction & frustration
In situations that have a direct connection to a drive, the narrator can let the person receive a penalty or bonus on dramatic or reasonable activities in the scene that promote or threaten the drive's fulfillment. The modification is linked to the drive's influence, so a subdued drive gives penalty/bonus (–1/+1), a burning (–2/+2) and a manic (–4/+4).

The modification applies to direct connections to fulfillment, preferably in such a way that the person can really imagine that the goal is within reach. This symbolizes the person's inner strength and will to complete something that has long been driving in his/her life.

Such a connection can also create frustration and a feeling of powerlessness in the scene if a person feels that the drive is close to being fulfilled but that something is hindering or disturbing. An example could be wanting to kill a person but when reaching him/her there are 300 witnesses nearby which makes it a physical or at least social suicide to fulfill the drive in a reasonable and credible way.

Example: Gabriel has the continuous drive "Protect Anya Malov (subdued)". In situations where he actually protects her from various misfortunes he receives a bonus (+1). In the same way he receives a penalty (–1) in situations where he experiences that he should protect Anya but cannot do so.

**Find strength**
A dazed or mortally wounded person, or one suffering from low Well-being, can in a scene where a drive is close to being fulfilled use this motivation to accomplish dramatic feats. It means that the person finds new strength in desperation, rises above the pain and anxiety and continues to fight to reach the goal of the drive.

The person can choose between rising and doing what he/she must do and for a short time focus on a single final task, or resignedly giving up under the injuries, bleeding, pain, exhaustion, poisoning, drug influence, hopelessness, despair or terror and just withdrawing, collapsing, admitting defeat and wasting away.

If the person chooses to pull themselves together to manage the final task, he/she temporarily counts their Well-being as Relaxed (10). Additionally, the person gets to act even if he/she would be dazed or mortally wounded. All other rules still apply as usual: the person dies from an additional Wound if he/she is already mortally wounded, the person dies when the rounds before death arrives have passed, and the total penalty the person has from their injuries applies.

Note that Find strength can only be activated for actions performed in direct purpose of fulfilling the drive. Should the person deviate from the goal the slightest bit, the effect disappears and the person can no longer act. When the person mentally releases the drive's focus, he/she can no longer resist the pain and fatigue or manage to push their body to extreme levels for any less noble purpose than the fulfillment of the drive. If the person in any way tries to leave the scene or perform any action that doesn't directly work for the fulfillment of the drive, the effects cease immediately.

Usually it only works to find strength from drives that were born before the current scene arose. This is to prevent someone from birthing a new drive as a response to an action that just occurred.

Example: The reporter Tremaro has the drive "Expose Franko Davidov", a shady businessman that Tremaro has written tons of articles about. Tremaro is now close to the big scoop. Find strength allows main characters (and also central narrator characters, if the narrator so wishes) to act even when the odds are against them. immediately gets to use one Fate point. The one who rescues the unfortunate one
may at most sacrifice ten points in this way. The one who is rescued
must not have their own Fate remaining.
Note that in the story it must be reasonable that
rescue can occur. For example, the person should be nearby
and notice the unfortunate one's predicament. The rescue
should occur in a dramatic and reasonable way in the story, perhaps
through an exchange of weary glances, an understanding nod, a
thumbs up, or something else that shows that a rescue
actually occurs.
Example: Nevei stands on a rooftop in the rain and observes the
mortally wounded Gabriel, who slowly feels his will to live draining from
him. Before Nevei stands Anya on her knees with Nevei's sword
blade against the side of her neck. Gabriel has no Fate left after
the wild battle that has unfolded and cannot act. Anya meets
Gabriel's dim gaze tearfully and forms a soundless "I love
you" with trembling lips. This is the signal for Anya to rescue
Gabriel (and hopefully herself) by expending
two Fate points so Gabriel can immediately use one. He

pistol toward Nevei, who is just lifting the blade. In the rain, with the pain

Survive (3-15 Fate points)
A person who knows he/she will probably be
injured can lower the number of Wounds the situation inflicts by one
per three Fate points (–1 Wound/3 Fate points). A maximum of 15
points can be expended in this way. The number of points must
be determined before the damage effect is confirmed, and even if
the number of Fate points would lower the number of Wounds to zero or
less, the victim always receives at least one Wound. If the situation results in
no Wounds being caused, the expended Fate is wasted.
How this occurs in the story must be described dramatically.
Survive can be used during executions (see page 271).
Example: Anya takes off with one of the witnesses to a bestial
murder with connections to one of the clans. The rain pours

an assassin who fires a shot at Anya. The shot
hits and Anya, who feels she must survive, sacrifices

three Wounds on her, which is reduced to zero. But since she must suffer
at least one Wound if Wounds arise, she still receives one Wound.

Play the odds (1-5 points, alt. 4 Fate points)
A person may expend Fate to increase their chances of succeeding
at difficulty and resistance rolls. This is usually called
"betting the odds". For each Fate point used, the person gets to roll
an extra die in the roll. A maximum of five points may be used in
a single roll. The player then chooses which two dice
results to use, other dice are ignored.
A player can also choose to spend four Fate points
to roll an extra die after a roll has been made.
This die can then be used to replace one of the
dice in the result, if desired.
Example: Mordekai fires snapshots at an axe-wielding
onrushing maniac. He must hit on the first shot as
he must make it to a meeting place to get information about
Janya's killer. A maximum of five Fate points are sacrificed. Seven
dice are rolled and the results are 1, 3, 4, 6, 7, 7, 10.
There are two sensible choices to make in this situation.
Either Mordekai chooses the two sevens and hopes that
it's enough to hit, in which case it becomes an exceptionally successful
roll (exceptionally failed if the result isn't sufficient).
Or he chooses one of the sevens and the ten which are maxed so
another die gets to be rolled. The five dice that aren't
chosen are ignored and removed from the roll.
Example: Jessca makes a difficulty roll (Stamina) to
manage to keep running. The dice show 1 and 1 — an exceptional
failure! This cannot happen as it's critical that she reaches
the goal in time. She sacrifices four Fate points and rolls one more die,
which shows 7. She removes one of the ones and replaces
it with the seven which means she succeeds with the roll.

What the various terms in the living standard template mean.
**Residence**
Indicates where/how the person lives.
**Assets**
Tells what assets are included in the person's household. Upon

the advancement cost. The previous assets disappear

Shows how much ready money the person has available in
the form of cash or quickly liquidated securities. All
personal belongings in chapter 3 are acquired with cash.
**Credit rating**
Shows how much the person can borrow without risking
losing a living standard. The loans come from family,

The advancement value indicates how much cash must
be invested to move up to the level from
the underlying level. It takes the value in the new living standard
in number of weeks to reach a new standard level.
Upon advancement, the person discards their old assets

not receive the cash specified in the new living standard but
retains the cash he/she had previously, after
the cash invested in the standard is deducted.
Debts, if any exist, are also retained.
A person may only move their living standard one level
at a time and may not skip any level.

A person can go into debt by borrowing money. When

**To fight**
Since Noir is an action-oriented game, the rules

The rules are made to be fast and support

In most cases when one person wants to attack another person,

combat. Initiative determines each involved person's turn order.

**Combat Actions**
Combat actions are what a person can perform during combat. There are two types of combat actions: initiative actions and defensive actions.

What actions mean & what they include
As players and narrator, you're meant to portray combat actions and bring them to life. Combat actions are described in the rules in fairly general terms. The intent is that the participant controlling the person who successfully performs a combat action describes what happens within the framework of the scene, story, and action.

Since combat actions often affect people other than oneself, it's also fair game to, within reasonable limits, affect other people during your own action. If a player's character shoots a narrator character, the narrator shouldn't prevent the player's description of what happens to the narrator character, even if it means they're moved or knocked down.

The fact that you can "take over" other people this way without problems works because they always come back on their Initiative in the turn order. The impact is purely dramatic and doesn't affect the mechanical scope for action in combat.

A combat action shouldn't be seen as a single individual maneuver but rather as an attitude toward what you want to accomplish and how. Many performed actions using firearms involve multiple rounds fired—this doesn't mean you go through how each individual bullet hits or misses but means the person has chosen to solve the task in a certain way, for example through lots of lead in the air.

**Movement during combat**
Most actions automatically include movement up to the person's Combat Movement, measured in meters, even if this isn't written out. This movement doesn't allow any advanced movement maneuvers. For those, use the initiative action Move.

A person may move around within their immediate area during their action to create atmosphere—for example, perhaps a person choosing to Fight runs around and around a conference table while chasing their target who's trying to escape (Defend). The total running distance becomes quite long, but the person doesn't move particularly far from their own starting point.

**Procedure**
Each time a combat action is performed, it follows a certain procedure, described here step by step.

1. The person with Initiative chooses an Initiative action.
2. If it's a difficulty roll to be made, do so. If it's an opposed roll to be made, the initiator determines who the target is. The target then chooses a defensive action in response. Both parties make rolls to determine who wins the opposed roll.
3. Initiative is modified based on the combat action's outcome and all other mechanics are noted immediately (such as ammunition expenditure or damage effects).
4. The person who succeeded with the combat action describes in a few sentences for the other participants what happens in the scene, within what's reasonable for the combat action.

**Ammunition Expenditure**
**Note that regardless of a combat action's outcome regarding firearms, the indicated amount of rounds is always consumed from the weapon's magazine.**

**It may seem like a lot of rounds are consumed during a firefight. That's correctly perceived, and entirely intentional. Most actions with firearms involve a greater or lesser amount of discharges. They're not intended to symbolize a systematic firing ("bang-bang-bang-bang-bang") but rather a stressed firing ("bangbangbang-bang-bangbang-bang-bang") because the actions themselves are so open to interpretation. For example, perhaps the combat action Snap Shots consumes nine shots in one action—this just means the situation for the shooter that round has involved multiple opportunities to fire. Most of the shots have probably missed the target. An automatic burst of perhaps 15 rounds can mean a longer burst in sequence or several shorter bursts—the shooter, scene, and situation determine this.**

**This is where the beauty of the open actions comes in—they're created to generate drama and action in a way that suits the story and scene. Both players and narrator are encouraged to take the opportunity to paint intense combat situations.**

What Does "Defend" Actually Mean?
Here are a number of interpretations of how it might look in the story when the defensive action Defend is used.
• An assassin fires burst shots from a machine pistol at Anya as she leaves police headquarters. She chooses to Defend, and wins against the attacker with her resistance roll. **Anya's player describes how Anya instinctively** senses the shooter coming up the stairs. As shots thunder in rapid succession, Anya rolls behind one of the large stone gargoyles standing at the top of the stairs. The statue is damaged and stone fragments break loose in the hail of bullets while Anya seeks cover.
• Gabriel creeps across a skyscraper rooftop with a pistol in hand. Through the rain he sees a figure approaching. It's Nevei, a Destroyer, who has been hunting Gabriel for several days. The confrontation is inevitable and Nevei attacks with a large ornate two-handed sword. Gabriel defends against the woman's thrust and manages to win the resistance roll. It's portrayed in the story as Nevei coming rushing forward screaming and Gabriel turning around in surprise. With rain lashing his face, Gabriel ducks just under the sword swing, rolls around, and comes up in a kneeling position with the pistol aimed at Nevei. Since Gabriel is next in turn order, it's dramatic that he gets the pistol up this way, and in his turn fires repeatedly at Nevei...
• Anya wakes with a start to someone kicking down her hotel door. In the hazy morning sunlight she sees hired scum that someone in the Gault family has sent to ensure her secret dies with her. Unabashed, the naked woman gets out of bed and meets the thug in close combat. She fights but the thug manages to defend. This is depicted by the narrator and player as her savagely delivering a kick but the thug getting his hand up, stopping the foot and twisting so that Anya is flung over and lands on a coffee table that breaks apart.
• Film star Sayah Bonn steps out of a taxi for the premiere of her new film. A crazed Creator in the crowd pulls out a shotgun. Sayah has time to perceive what's happening and defends. She manages to roll better than the shooter. In the story nothing more happens than Sayah having time to twist enough that the shotgun blast only tears apart her wide red ball gown and cape with a boom.
• Mordekai is assaulted by a junkie who turns out to be one of the Afflicted. The junkie strikes with a cobra's speed and begins pummeling Mordekai, who defends and succeeds. The situation is portrayed in the story as Mordekai perceiving what's happening just in time, ducking under the punch, circling around, avoiding a kick by jumping over some old crates and parrying a flurry of strikes (remember that the initiative action fight doesn't show a single blow, but only that the person is fighting in close combat to defeat their opponent) before the event is over.

In the story it's determined that the guard perceives what's happening and just barely gets a trash can lid up as a shield against the knife, which is deflected and lands some distance away.

None of the situations above are described in the rules, beyond what can be found under the defensive action Defend. It's the players and/or narrator who together determine how combat situations manifest in the world. The intention is to create dynamics and tension in the scene. That participants around the gaming table use their imagination collectively and partake in the rich gaming experience Noir is meant to be.

Gladly let tough, bold and spectacular events occur, let there be tension in the story and don't worry if someone else determines something that affects your protagonist or narrator character — after all, Initiative determines when a person gets to act and there are essentially no situations where this is prevented. Thus almost anything can happen in the world — even if it's breakneck or life-threatening things.

Physical Injuries & Death
It's more or less inevitable that a person suffers violence in Sandukar's dark alleys and abandoned basement spaces. The injuries caused vary from minor scrapes, scratches and bruises, to serious physical injuries that in the worst case are fatal but give the soul a peace that cannot be found in the world of the living.

**Physical Injuries**
When a person is injured in Noir, they become either lightly or seriously injured.

Light injuries result in the victim receiving Disorientation but are not life-threatening and one recovers from them fairly quickly. However, Disorientation is debilitating and can cause the person to become temporarily knocked out or unconscious.

**Serious injuries give the victim both Disorientation** and Wounds. Wounds can kill the victim and it takes a long time to recover from them. Moreover, Wounds are severely debilitating.

Damage & Wound Threshold
All dangerous situations that risk injuring a person cause damage, based on the situation's, or weapon's, damage value.

Damage is obtained by rolling the number of dice specified as the attack's damage value. The result of the roll is added to the victim's Disorientation, which shows the immediate disorientation, pain experience, stress and shock that the injury gives rise to.

If damage is also higher than the victim's attribute value in Wound Threshold, the victim receives a Wound — the damage was so serious that the victim risks dying from it.

If damage is higher than the victim's double attribute value in Wound Threshold, the victim instead receives as many Wounds as the number the lowest die in the damage roll shows. If damage is higher than the victim's quadruple attribute value in Wound Threshold, the victim instead receives as many Wounds as the value the highest die in the damage roll shows. Note that the number of Wounds does not in any way show how many individual injuries a person has suffered. A single instance of damage can result in the victim receiving loads of Wounds. Rather, see the number of Wounds as an indicator of how seriously injured the victim is.

**Subduing Damage**
There are certain attacks or situations that inflict so-called subduing damage. The difference between subduing and regular, lethal, damage is that the former has a harder time inflicting Wounds on the victim — the victim temporarily doubles their value in Wound Threshold at the moment of injury.

What causes subduing damage is primarily brawls, blunt force, shockwaves, attacks with weapons lacking a cutting edge or penetrating point, as well as fall and collision damage.

Weapons or attacks that deal subduing damage are marked by a "B" in front of the damage value, for example "B1D+1".

Endurance & Disorientation
A victim who receives increased Disorientation must make a difficulty roll (Endurance). The roll is only affected by the penalties from the total amount of Disorientation the victim has.

Exceptionally successful: The victim can continue to act, despite their injuries.
Successful: The victim can continue to act, despite their injuries.
Failed: The victim is disoriented and may attempt to recover in their turn each round. Initiative is set to one (1).
Exceptionally failed: The victim is unconscious for as many minutes as the total amount of Disorientation they have.

**Disoriented**
If the victim becomes disoriented, this means in the scene that the victim is temporarily knocked out, stunned, or severely confused.

While the victim is disoriented, they may not perform any actions whatsoever, not even scream or attempt to call attention.

A disoriented person automatically receives Initiative one (1) and is counted as defenseless, see page 271. A disoriented person who receives additional Disorientation automatically becomes unconscious.

**Recover**
A disoriented person may make a difficulty roll (Endurance) on their turn each round to recover. Penalties come only from any Disorientation.

Exceptionally successful: The victim recovers and immediately gets to raise their Initiative as many steps as the value from all dice. In the next round they may act as normal.
Successful: The victim recovers. In the next round they may act as normal.
Failed: The victim is still disoriented and may attempt to recover again next round.
Exceptionally failed: The victim becomes unconscious for as many minutes as the total amount of Disorientation they have.

**Unconscious**
An unconscious victim may not perform any actions whatsoever. The victim wakes on their own after as many minutes as the value in Disorientation was at the moment unconsciousness took effect.

An unconscious person is always counted as defenseless, see page 271.

Wake the Unconscious
A medically trained person can attempt to wake an unconscious person. If the medically trained person succeeds at a difficulty roll (Medicine), the unconscious person is instead counted as disoriented. Each such attempt takes one round. Appropriate methods include slaps, cold water or chemical smelling salts.

**Summary of Being Injured**
1. Determine damage from the attack (from weapon, attack type or event) by rolling the dice indicated at the weapon's damage value. Let the dice remain visible on the table.
2. Increase the victim's amount of **Disorientation by as much as** the damage dice show.
3. If damage is higher than the victim's Wound Threshold, the victim receives one Wound. If damage is higher than the victim's double Wound Threshold, the victim receives as many wounds as the lowest die in the damage roll shows. If damage is higher than the victim's quadruple Wound Threshold, the victim receives as many wounds as the highest die in the damage roll shows.
4. The victim makes a difficulty roll (Endurance). If the roll fails, the victim is disoriented and must recover to be able to act again. A disoriented victim's Initiative is set to 1.
5. If the victim received one or more Wounds, they must make a difficulty roll (Will to Live). If the roll fails, the victim is counted as mortally wounded and dies after as many rounds as the sum of the difficulty roll's result, and initiative is set to 1. **If the difficulty roll failed** exceptionally, the victim dies immediately. A mortally wounded victim must be stabilized to survive.

**Defenseless Victims**
A person considered defenseless is very vulnerable. To be considered defenseless requires that the person is unaware that danger is imminent, such as during an unexpected ambush, a sniper attack with a rifle from long range, or a punch in the face they're not at all prepared for. It can also involve situations where the person is unable to protect themselves, for example they might be tied up, pinned down, sleeping, unconscious, or subjected to gunpoint.

People who are dazed due to failed checks (Stamina) are also considered defenseless, see page 263, as are mortally wounded persons.

A person who reasonably is prepared that an attack might come, for example if gunfire has been noticed or threats have occurred, and who can act without hindrance almost never counts as defenseless (however, the person can be considered surprised, see page 254).

There are two actions an attacker can perform against a defenseless victim — Knock Out and Execute.

**Knock Out**
A person can attempt to knock out a defenseless person so they become unconscious or incapacitated. This requires a difficulty check (Melee), (Agility), or (Strength) for this — the situation determines which skill fits best. If the attempt is performed with some form of implement (for example a pistol butt, blackjack, wooden club, etc.) a bonus (+5) is given to the roll. Note that only the outcome from this roll inflicts damage on the target — the damage value is ignored.

Exceptionally successful: The victim's Daze increases by ten times the sum from all dice (all dice x 10) and they automatically become unconscious for as many minutes as the total amount of Daze they have.
Successful: The victim's Daze increases by ten times the sum from all dice (all dice x 10) and must roll a difficulty check (Stamina), see page 263.
Failed: The victim's Daze increases by ten times the value of the highest die (highest die x 10) and must roll a difficulty check (Stamina), see page 263.
Exceptionally failed: The victim's Daze increases by the value of the lowest die and must roll a difficulty check (Stamina), see page 263. The attacker's Wellbeing deteriorates (−1), see page 242.

Example: Anya has gotten hold of a snitch who knows about Rulov's illegal trade in war slaves. She has gotten him to lead her to the place where the slaves are being prepared to serve their new masters, and when the snitch turns his back, she pulls out her revolver and strikes the butt against the back of his neck.

The narrator judges that since the attack was unexpected and comes from behind, the snitch doesn't get to attempt to defend himself at all but counts as defenseless. The difficulty check (Strength) (which was the skill that felt most reasonable in the situation) succeeds and the dice show 5 and 10. Another die is rolled since the 10 was maxed — it shows 6, sum 5 + 10 + 6 = 21 x 10 = 210. The snitch receives 210 in Daze and rolls his check (Stamina), with a penalty (−21). The roll fails. The snitch collapses dazed onto the rain-wet asphalt.

**Execute**
The narrator must determine whether a person has the capacity to execute another, based on personality and circumstances, as an execution is an exceptionally cold-blooded and mentally taxing act. Additionally, requirements for some form of suitable implement may exist, although a skilled brawler can execute a defenseless person with their bare hands, through for example strangulation, suffocation, neck-breaking, or simply massive violence.

For a sniper assassination, the right kind of weapon for the purpose is required, and the shooter must succeed in hitting despite the distance (the victim then gets to benefit from a chance defense, see page 260).

Attempting to execute a defenseless person requires a difficulty check (Resolve). If the attempt is performed with some form of ranged weapon (for example a revolver) a bonus (+5) is given, as this is not as personal. Note that the outcome from the roll inflicts the only damage on the target — the damage value is ignored.

Exceptionally successful: The executioner is ice-cold and the victim is inflicted with as many Wounds as the value of the highest die in the roll. The victim must roll a difficulty check (Willpower), see page 264.
Successful: The executioner carries out their task efficiently and the victim is inflicted with as many Wounds as the value of the lowest die in the roll. The victim must roll a difficulty check (Willpower), see page 264.
Failed: The executioner performs the action with great hesitation and nervousness, and risks being affected by stress equivalent to a shocking experience, see page 244. The victim suffers one Wound. The victim must roll a difficulty check (Willpower), see page 264.
Exceptionally failed: The person fails catastrophically with the execution and risks being affected by stress equivalent to psychological trauma, see page 244.

**Defenseless Victims in Combat**
**Situations where a defenseless victim** ends up in regular combat can very well arise, for example if a person presses a revolver muzzle against the victim's temple or holds a stiletto against their throat. If the defenseless person wants to start a fight and thus try to take control of the situation, difficulty checks (Overview) are rolled just as in normal cases when a fight starts. However, the defenseless victim receives a penalty (−5) on their roll since they find themselves at a disadvantage.

**The defenseless person counts as** defenseless until they have chosen their first Initiative action. This means that if they get a lower initiative than their opponent, the opponent can still choose to execute or knock out the defenseless person instead of performing a normal Initiative action. Thus, it is very risky to start fighting as defenseless, but sometimes it might be one's only chance.

Noir is a dystopian roleplaying game written by two veterans of Swedish RPG
development, Marco Behrmann and Petter Nallo. Between them they have nearly 30 years
of experience in roleplaying and are behind titles like Eon, Neotech and Viking.
Noir explores humanity's darkest sides and is not recommended for sensitive individuals or for children under 15 years.
